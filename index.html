When an eating disorder needs medical care, not just more referrals.

A practical pathway if you or someone you love or care for may be medically unsafe, very underweight, losing weight quickly, or being sent home without a clear eating disorder care plan.

BMI around or under 16, and especially around or under 14, should be treated as a serious medical risk signal that needs urgent medical assessment.

Private psychologists or eating disorder clinics may not accept a person straight away, or may not be the right first step, until medical assessment or stabilisation has been considered.

Immediate danger?

Call 000 or go to the nearest Emergency Department if there are signs of medical collapse, severe weakness, fainting, chest pain, confusion, self-harm risk, dehydration, electrolyte concern, or inability to eat or drink safely.

This site does not give medical advice. BMI alone does not decide risk. A person may need urgent medical assessment because of physical signs, psychiatric risk, rate of weight loss, blood results, heart observations, or inability to maintain nutrition.

Do not wait for a psychology appointment

Severe restriction can become a medical emergency.

Very low BMI is not just a counselling issue. If you or someone you love or care for has a BMI around 16 or below, is losing weight quickly, fainting, struggling to eat or drink, or feels medically unsafe, this may need urgent medical assessment at hospital.

Eating disorder care pathways

Find the right care contact.

An eating disorder care coordinator helps join up care when it feels scattered. They can help work out who to contact, connect the GP, hospital, mental health team and eating disorder services, and make the next steps clearer so the person is not left with separate referrals and no plan.

Words you can use

  • "I am worried my eating disorder is making my body unsafe."
  • "Please check whether I am medically safe to go home."
  • "I need a clear eating disorder plan, not just another referral."
  • "Who is the person I contact if things get worse after I leave?"

Professional pathway

Four checks before the person leaves care.

1

Medical safety assessment

Assess eating disorder medical risk directly, including observations, weight trajectory, intake, purging, hydration, bloods and ECG where clinically indicated.

2

Care coordination responsibility

Identify the clinician, service or coordinator responsible for the next step, and document how the GP, hospital, mental health and specialist teams connect.

3

Discharge planning

Confirm the written plan covers nutrition, refeeding risk, follow-up timing, responsible contacts and clear return-to-ED instructions.

4

Professional escalation

Escalate through ED, medical registrar, specialist eating disorder service, local RCRP coordinator or senior clinical review if risk is unresolved.
